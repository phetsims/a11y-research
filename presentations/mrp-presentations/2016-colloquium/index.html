Inclusive Design Research Colloquium, OCAD University Jan. 21, 2016

Designing Inclusive Supports for
Non-visual Use of a Highly Interactive Simulation

Advisory Committee

  • Emily Moore &
  • Clayton Lewis

Interactive Simulations, Key part of Science education

  • Due to increasing availability of computer devices, the use of computer simulations in the classroom has become widespread (Rutten, van Joolingen, & van der Veen, 2012)
  • Computer-based simulations allow students to use models to investigate scientific phenomena in different physical scales and timelines, and when when real equipment is not available (D’Angelo et al., 2014)
  • Simulations have a beneficial effect on student learning (D’Angelo et al., 2014; Rutten et al., 2012)

PhET Interactive Simulations

  • Carl Wieman (Nobel Prize in Physics, 2001).
  • 75 million downloads a year.
  • High quality simulations - based in research.
  • Low on accessibility – highly visual and designed for mouse and touch interaction.
  • Consider a rudimentary experiment in Balloons and Static Electricity.
  • Now consider, how a person cannot see & who does not use a mouse might engage with this simulation?

Goals: Part of a larger project

My Research Focus

  • Exploring inclusive design solutions that provide non-visual access to a student using a keyboard and screen reader.
  • How can the learning experience of students with vision loss using a PhET simulation be made accessible, engaging and effective?

Target User

Students with vision loss who engage with a computer with a screen reader and keyboard.

  • Understandable & timely dynamic verbal text description,
  • Intuitive keyboard interactions that engage the user with the content,
  • Immersive sound effects, or sonification.

Keeping in mind: a design where students can collaboration.

Electronic Curb cuts: access for visual keyboard users, switch users.

Literature & Research Background

  • Pulling from: Web standards, how blind people use the web, HCI, Gaming & Education
  • Guidelines & design patterns exist for typical web applications (online forms or a shopping site).
  • Description guidelines for static images & simple dynamic graphics (Barstow, McKell, Rothberg, & Schmidt, 2004; Keane & Laverent, 2014)

PhET sims are unique

  • No well defined path to success, because learning is more diffuse.
  • Highly interactive.

Prototype thus far provides

  • Parallel-DOM creates an accessible content layer
  • Keyboard interactions provide a way to navigate & interact with content
  • ARIA live regions (web standards) update the content layer dynamically as the students interact & screen readers then deliver the updates simultaneously in real time.

Initial findings from 4 interviews

Navigational strategies

P1 (Ross): "I generally use the arrow keys to navigate line by line."
P4: [Starts off by navigating by section] "Nice, lots of regions...now, I'll look for the Main region." [Finds it on second go around.]

Engagement with science

P3: [with 2 negatively charged balloons on the Sweater] "I want to see what happens if I put one balloon on top of the other." [second Balloon repels and moves to the bottom of the sweater.] "What will happen if I do the same thing on the Wall?"

Complex problem space

  • How people who are blind approach interactive simulations running in a browser.
    • Like typical web pages & employ known navigational strategies
  • Authentic, unprompted exploration & discovery by the participants
    • Indicates that the approach we're taking, thus far with content & keyboard interactions can result in access, engagement and self-directed learning for diverse users.

Future Work & Next Steps

  • Refinement, optimization of dynamic descriptions.
  • Explore simple immersive sonifications for rubbing, bumping into wall, some charge behaviour.
